Explain what you’re interested in

In this session we investigate the philosophical traditions and theories philosophers devoted in. The two subjects, however, are ambiguously defined and come with diverse categories. It is not our goal to find a best way to sort things out, so we combined multiple resources trying to cover as many nodes as possible.

  • The term “philosophical traditions” usually refer to the style of approaches one philosopher applies when probing into his interested topics. Traditions are related to time and place. Wikipedia Category: Philosophers by tradition provides lists of philosophers in these 33 traditions:

Explain the tool

a. Degree Analysis

  • We provided a general overview of how many nodes each tradition/theory contains by a histogram, and selected the ones that exceeded 20 nodes for further analysis.
  • We then looked into the degree features inside and outside each tradition/theory community. Because the size varies from one to another, we calculated the average degree instead of the total link number. We calculated 3 features to reveal the characteristics: the average links completely inside that community, the average links pointing to the outside, and the average links starting from outside and pointing to the inside. A line-plot illustrates the results.

b. Bipartite Graph

To find the inter-relations between traditions/theories, we further built up some weighted Bipartite graphs to represent the connection in between. The traditions/theories are new nodes, and directed links are drawn with their linewidth scaled according to (1)the total links number and (2)normalized by the community size.

c. Significant Figures

We sorted nodes in each tradition/theory community both by in-degree and out-degree, and chose the top 3 from each for further analysis.

d. Textual Analysis

We downloaded some works from Internet of the significant figures found in the previous step, and did a regular tf-idf analysis, to see if we can extract some key words that specify each tradition/theory. We generated wordclouds to visualize results from word frequency and calculated tf-idf, and inspect the top words for each tradition/theory.

Results and discussion

Tradition

a. Degree Analysis

  • Philosophers are not evenly distributed through all traditions. About 1/5 fall in “Philosophers of science”, which makes up the largest tradition community in our network. Other mainstream traditions like Analytic and Scholastic are no doubt in the top, and so is the case with Eastern philosophy as a specific branch.
  • Besides, Exisitentialism, as a major sub-category in Continental, also comes with a significant size.
  • Many ancient traditions are less involved in our network, like Platonist and Cyrenaic. Some very modern traditions such as Posthumanism are also not very “popular” in our network.

  • The inner avg degree descibes the extent of connenctions inside each tradition community. Marxists, Analytic philosophers and Philosophers of science are the most united community. Their academic development seems to be highly interactive with their peers.
  • Critical theorists, as their name suggests, have the lowest inner avg degree but very high inside-outside avg degree. They are criticals and yes they criticize!
  • By observing all 3 degree features, Philosophers of science, Existentialists, Marxists, Continental philosophers seem to be the more “active communities” in philosophy. They have frequent interactions both with themselves and the outside, and are mentioned by other communities very often.

b. Trandition Bipartite

In our network, the following edge paths seem to occur most often:

(Analytic philosophers, Philosophers of science)
(Philosophers of science, Analytic philosophers)
(Philosophers of science, Existentialists)
(Existentialists, Philosophers of science)

and the following edge paths are completely absent:

(Eastern philosophers, Analytic philosophers)
(Eastern philosophers, Continental philosophers)
(Eastern philosophers, Critical theorists)
(Scholastic philosophers, Marxists)
(Scholastic philosophers, Critical theorists)
(Critical theorists, Eastern philosophers)
(Critical theorists, Scholastic philosophers）

  • The activeness of Philosophers of science and Existentialists is consistent with the previous findings. Besides this, Analytic philosophers and Philosophers of science are highly interactive with each other. Since the average inter-degrees for the Analytic philosophers isn’t very high (outside-inside: 6.547945, inside-outside: 6.712329), it is therefore possible that there is some key figure bridging the two community.
  • Eastern philosophers are a fairly isolated community based on geographical and historical reasons. They usually have different philosophical concerns rising from different social institution, and have restricted access of literal communication with other groups because of language barriers.
  • Though (Continental philosophers, Eastern philosophers) is 0, we can find Existentialists, the sub-caterory of Continental, link to Eastern philosophers.

We also normalized the link counts by each tradition’s node number and plotted the results in the second weighted Bipartite graph. The normalized link number represents the average link number a node in one tradtion have to a node in another tradtion. Surprisingly the most possible edge paths are:

(Critical theorists, Philosophers of science) - 2.476190
(Existentialists, Philosophers of science) - 2.451613
(Analytic philosophers, Philosophers of science) - 2.068493
(Idealists, Philosophers of science) - 1.818182
(Philosophers of science, Analytic philosophers) - 1.435644
(Scholastic philosophers, Philosophers of science) - 1.270833
(Marxists, Philosophers of science) - 1.258065
(Continental philosophers, Marxists) - 1.192308

The weights above can be interpreted as a “possibility” of a node in one tradition links to a node in aother tradition. It is not surprising that Philosophers of science is a tradition most nodes would link to, but it is also interesting to find that Continental philosophers are very likely to link to Marxists. Considering that Continental tradition rised up in the 19-20th century, and meanwhile Marxism also developed and flourished, this result proves the high interaction between the two traditions in that specific period.

d. Textual Analysis

Wordcloud

The wordcloud graphs show the top 50 words scaled by their word count.

What they have in common:

  • The word ‘one’ appears to prevail through most of the traditions. Considering the style of the discourses we used, that most of them are argumentative proses, it is required to avoid subjective pronouns by using “one” instead.
  • In argumentative discourses, modal verbs like “must”, “may”, “could” and “would” are also heavily used.
  • The word ‘god’ is stressed in Analytic, Scholastic and Existentialists. These traditions were either built on religious purposes or tried to overturn the religious trend of its time.

What are different, i.e. words for specification:

  • Philosophers of science: “nature”, “reason”
  • Analytic philosophers: “sense”, “world”, “god”, “knowledge”
  • Eastern philosophers: “master”, “people”, “chap”
    Because we used The Sayings Of Confucius to build our corpus, and this is a book recording conversations between Confucius and his students, “master” is frequently used for students to call their teacher.
  • Scholastic philosophers: “god”, “man”, “things”
  • Existentialists: “life”, “god”, “world”, “love”, “man”
    This is the only group emphasizing love! (Actually Continental philosophers also do so, but not as much as Existentialists). No wonder Existentialists were once seen as the romanticism hippies.
  • Marxists: “consciousness”, “ego”, “order”, “bourgeois”, “class”
  • Continental philosophers: “state”, “land”, “free”
  • Idealists: “history”, “vico”, “science”, “economic”, “historical” “vico” comes from the book The Philosophy of Giambattista Vico which is a name of another philosopher. But it is interesting to find out Idealists actually heavily use realistic words like “history”, “science”, “economic” and “historical”. Maybe Idealists are on the contrary the most realistic ones.
  • Critical theorists: “space”, “production”, “social”, “political”, “nature”

Theory

a. Degree Analysis

  • Before any theory analysis starts, it is important to notice that we have fewer philosophers who have valid theory information, and this makes all the following analysis less representative of the real-life situation.
  • We have one largest community of theories– scholastic philosophers, and several less largest communities of comparable sizes: atheist philosophers, utilitarians, ancient Platonists, pragmatists and secular humanists.
  • The theories containing over 20 nodes are selected for further analysis. They are scholastic philosophers, atheist philosophers, utilitarians, ancient Platonists, pragmatists.

  • All theories have roughly the same inner avg degree, in other words similar extent of interaction with their colleagues.
  • But scholastic philosophers , even though the largest community, are not often referred to by other communities. The inter-degrees are both low.
  • Utilitarians and ancient Platonists are referred to more than they refer to others.

b. Theory Bipartite

In our network, the following edge paths occur most often:

(atheist philosophers, utilitarians)
(scholastic philosophers, ancient Platonists)
(pragmatists, atheist philosophers)

  • Athiests and utilitarians both rejects subjects of low functionality in the world, and this explains why many atheists link to utilitarians with similar ideas.
  • Scholastic philosophers mostly live in early historical stages, and ancient Platonists are their main source of reference.
  • Pragmatism is a modern philosophical trend that blended various concerns to inspect their practical uses and successes. It developed a unique religious stance (neither completely accept god nor deny its existence), which hallmarks the trend. It is therefore not surprising they interact with atheist philosophers very much.

The second normalized Bipartite graph illustrates the average link number a node in one theory links to a node in aother theory. Only pragmatists-atheist philosophers exceeds 1, indicating the graph is far from fully connected.

d. Textual Analysis

Wordcloud

Words in common:

  • Pronouns “one” and “man” are very common in the text we chose to avoid subjective narrative in argumentative discourses.
  • Modal verbs, “may”, “must”, “would”, “shall” are also traces of how argumentative discourses avoid subjectivity.

Feature words for specification:

  • scholastic philosophers: “things”, “reply”, “sin”
  • atheist philosophers: “writing”, “thus”, “thou”
  • utilitarians: “capital”, “labor”, “money”, “government”
    Key words for utilitarians are actually quite the same as the marxists. Actually there were indeed controversies on whether Marxism is also Utilitarianism. Contemporary scholars Derek Allen and George Brenkert have posted different opinions.
  • ancient Platonists: “good”, “things”, “devine”
  • pragmatists: “life”, “world”, “us”, “mind”
    Pragmatists appear to be the most realistic community by stressing “life” and “mind”. They therefore share some in common with existentialists, and it’s true that they also have some overlapping period in time.
