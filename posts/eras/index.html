In this part we look at our network from a temporal perspective. We have 9 different timelines: ancient Greece (‘ancientGreek’), pre-christ (‘centuriesBC’), first to tenth centuries (‘1stthrough10thcenturies’), eleventh to fourteenth centuries (‘11ththrough14thcenturies’), fifteenth and sixteenth centuries (‘15thand16thcenturies’), seventeenth century (‘17thcentury’), eighteenth century (‘18thcentury’), nineteenth century (‘19thcentury’), and twentieth century (‘20thcentury’).

We will try to characterize as much as possible each of these periods and see if we can highlight some patterns.

Connections inside and outside each era :

Can we determine what are the most significant eras regarding the edges ?

For this we need to look at the inside and outside edges. For one era are there more connections inside this era or outside ?

As we see here we can detect some patterns. First of all, it is normal that the proportion of outside links going inside for more recent eras is lower. This is because modern philosophers refer to older philosophers (and not the other way around). Nevertheless, this plausible hypothesis is not entirely verified.

  • We notice first of all that for the BC philosophers the number of internal links is greater than for the other eras and that moreover the proportion of links from other era is great. This shows that this period was very rich in philosophy. Philosophers exchanged more during this period (inside links) and moreover they were the most influential later on and all this with a rather low number of philosophers.

  • After that there was no great increase in the number of philosophers. Until the 14th century the number of internal links and external references decreased. These times are probably marked by a difficulty to do philosophy because of a limited freedom of expression.

  • It is from the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries onwards that philosophy takes a new lease of life. We notice that the number of references to the interior of these periods increases greatly (always in proportion) and that there are more and more external references until the eighteenth century. We can see a growing evolution of philosophy, which first relies on studies that have already been carried out and which gradually emancipates itself to become in the eighteenth century very influential throughout the other philosophical eras.

  • From the nineteenth century onwards we see an explosion in the number of philosophers. The easier access to a more advanced education allowed the emergence of new issues in philosophies. This results in dense sub-graphs with fewer links to other eras

Bag of words analysis :

Each philosophical era is characterised by very different issues. The study of the importance and the specificity of each word according to the periods shows a certain evolution of the philosophical stakes.

  • Before christ:

  • Between the first and the 10th centuries :

The first thing we notice is that each bag of words contains important non-philosophers (as we deleted them) for each era. Before Christ and until the 10th century these people are important emperors (Antoninus) and great religious figures (Ennead and Nāgārjuna). Despite this, Before Christ we find the presence of scientific terms such as Pythagora or Hippocratic which already shows a close link between science and philosophy. From the first century onwards we do not find any particular theme but a lot of names of different emperors (Antoninus, Constantius, Theodosius) and different deities (nagargune, ennead). This shows that philosophy was mainly at the service of political and religious leaders.

Between the 17th and 18th century we see a strong transition. In the 17th century we still have an important influence of religion with the English bishop Andrewes and immaterialism (philosophical current) but in the 18th century this is no longer the case at all! In the 18th century the word science would appear twice (once in English and once in German). The words ‘political’, ‘social’ and ‘branch’ (which refer to Stendhal and an allegory of love) show a generalisation of the issues. The presence of ‘american’ and ‘Burr’ (American politician) also shows that philosophy is spreading across the Atlantic.

After the 18th century the transition to social and scientific fields explodes. The 19th and 20th centuries show the strong political contradictions of the time. The words capitalist, bolshevik, socialist, political occupy a major part of these two bags of words. We also find other political considerations like proletariat and anarchism. The sciences also play a very important role, with ‘Galileo’ highlighting the fracture observed with religious themes in relation to other eras.
